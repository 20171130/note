1. Less is more.
 When proposing or evaluating a new idea, strip features, designs, and domain specialization down to the simplest, most general core — we often carry redundant details from familiarity or initial framing. A good idea can be writen on the back of a business card.
- Prefer general-purpose tools over specialized ones — e.g. git + markdown + vscode + python > Obsidian with plugins > Notion.

2. Gather information
Discuss with others for advice, at least should be able to explain the idea clearly and preferably convince somebody.
Search for relevant work, if the need is real and if there is already a good solution.
Think then gather information, not the opposite order — so not preoccupied by existing literature.

3. Innovation and monopoly
Classic economics: Monopoly lead to profit, competition drives net profit to zero. Innovation is the only good kind of monopoly: from zero to one (Peter Thiel's from zero to one is my favoriate book on business), instead of from multi to one. An idea is only good if other people have not worked hard enough on it.

4. Find a niche and PMF
A general plot needs a special start point, Amazon started as book store, paypal started for ebay. find the niche where it works best. A few fanatic loyal customers is better than many potential ones.

4. The moat of metastability
The most stable kind of monopoly is not moat of technology, which will eventually vanish when the fundemental science and technology converge: even Intel lost it after decades of being much better than AMD, as Moore's law converges. The most stable one is ecosystem: one user use wechat because his friends use wechat, although wechat may not be a good product on its own. like a metastable state, each spin is up because other spins are up. It takes a large potential barrier for all users to flip together.

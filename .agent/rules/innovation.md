1. Less is more.
When proposing or evaluating a new idea, strip features, designs, and domain specialization down to the simplest, most general core — we often carry redundant details from familiarity or initial framing. A good idea fits on the back of a business card.
- Prefer general-purpose tools over specialized ones — e.g. git + markdown + vscode + python > Obsidian with plugins > Notion.

2. Gather information.
Discuss with others for advice; at minimum be able to explain the idea clearly, and preferably convince somebody.
Search for relevant work to check whether the need is real and whether a good solution already exists.
Think first, gather information second — so you are not preoccupied by existing literature.

3. Innovation and monopoly.
Classic economics: monopolies earn profit; competition drives net profit to zero. Innovation is the only good kind of monopoly — from zero to one (Peter Thiel's *Zero to One* is my favorite book on business), not from many to one. An idea is only good if other people have not worked hard enough on it.

4. Find a niche and PMF.
A general path needs a specific starting point: Amazon started as a bookstore, PayPal started for eBay. Find the niche where it works best. A few fanatic loyal customers beat many potential ones.

5. The moat of metastability.
The most stable monopoly is not a technological moat — that vanishes once the underlying science and technology converge; even Intel lost its lead after decades ahead of AMD as Moore's law converged. The most stable moat is the ecosystem: one user uses WeChat because their friends use WeChat, even if WeChat is not the best product on its own. Like a metastable state, each spin stays up because the surrounding spins are up; it takes a large potential barrier for all users to flip together.
